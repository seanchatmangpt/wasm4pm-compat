\chapter{Multi-Agent Adversarial Evaluation}
\label{ch:evaluation}

The manufacturing doctrine demands that certification claims be earned, not narrated. Chapter~\ref{ch:evaluation}
records the structured multi-agent adversarial evaluation conducted against the
\texttt{wasm4pm-compat} codebase at the PARTIAL\_003 checkpoint, the defects
found under each evaluation dimension, the corrective actions taken, and the
synthesis verdict that gates the PARTIAL~$\to$~ALIVE\_004 transition.

%% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:eval-methodology}

Five independent agents evaluated the codebase along orthogonal dimensions.
Each agent operated without access to the other agents' drafts during the
evaluation phase; verdicts were submitted as structured records before
synthesis. A synthesis agent received all five records and produced the
composite score and critical-path determination.

\subsection{Agent Roles}

\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{TypeSystemArchitect}]
    Examines the const-generic type-law kernel: whether nightly feature usage
    is justified and consistent, whether non-forgeability seals are sound,
    whether \texttt{Evidence<T, State, W>} and \texttt{WfNetConst<SOUNDNESS>}
    maintain their invariants across all public surfaces, and whether
    compile-fail fixtures reject for the \emph{intended named law} rather than
    accidentally.

  \item[\textbf{ProcessMiningScholar}]
    Evaluates coverage of the Van~der~Aalst canon: whether the paper ledger's
    81-paper corpus is correctly classified, whether every key formal object
    (WF-net, OCEL tuple, process tree, Declare constraint, DFG, POWL) has a
    faithful Rust type surface, and whether domain boundary decisions
    (graduation vs.\ structure-only) are principled.

  \item[\textbf{ManufacturingQualityInspector}]
    Audits the receipt infrastructure: fixture annotation density, receipt
    class coverage across commit history, absence of compile-fail fixtures that
    pass for the wrong reason, and integrity of the ALIVE gate audit mesh.

  \item[\textbf{StrategicDirector}]
    Assesses gap closure velocity, the PARTIAL checkpoint discipline, whether
    residuals are named precisely or vaguely, and whether the ALIVE\_004 target
    is achievable on the critical path derived from the defects found by the
    other four agents.

  \item[\textbf{AdversarialRedTeam}]
    Attempts to defeat the type law: forging witnessed soundness, bypassing the
    admission gate, creating cross-witness confusion, inflating the paper
    ledger, and producing compile-fail fixtures whose \texttt{.stderr} records
    absence proofs (E0425) rather than type-law verdicts (E0308).
    A lower score means the codebase held under more adversarial attempts; a
    score of $3/10$ means the Red Team found and confirmed three actionable
    attack surfaces out of ten probed.
\end{description}

\section{Type System Defects Found}
\label{sec:eval-typesystem}

\subsection{WfNet Split-Brain Non-Forgeability}

The TypeSystemArchitect identified a split-brain defect in the soundness
hierarchy. Two parallel types model WF-net soundness:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{WfNet<S>} --- uses uninhabited marker types
    (\texttt{SoundnessUnknown}, \texttt{SoundnessClaimed},
    \texttt{SoundnessWitnessed}) as the \texttt{S} phantom parameter.
  \item \texttt{WfNetConst<\{SOUNDNESS: SoundnessState\}>} --- uses
    \texttt{adt\_const\_params} to make the soundness state a const-generic
    parameter, enforcing non-forgeability via a private \texttt{WfNetSeal}
    inner type.
\end{itemize}

The defect: \texttt{WfNet<SoundnessUnknown>::attest\_witnessed} is freely
callable from any call site, transitioning a net to
\texttt{WfNet<SoundnessWitnessed>} without presenting any proof token. No
admission gate is enforced; the typestate transition is free. This makes
\texttt{WfNet} a \emph{forgeable} soundness carrier, violating the
non-forgeability invariant stated in the architecture chapter.

\texttt{WfNetConst<\{SoundnessState::Witnessed\}>}, by contrast, can only be
constructed via \texttt{WfNetConst::witness\_soundness}, which requires a
\texttt{SoundnessProof} token. \texttt{SoundnessProof} wraps a
\texttt{wfnet\_seal::WfNetSeal} whose constructor is \texttt{pub(super)},
preventing construction outside the \texttt{petri} module. This is the
non-forgeable path.

\paragraph{Corrective action.}
\texttt{WfNet::attest\_witnessed} was deprecated with the annotation:

\begin{quote}
\textit{``use WfNetConst which enforces non-forgeability:
WfNetConst::witness\_soundness requires a SoundnessProof token that is only
constructible inside petri or via the wasm4pm graduation bridge.
WfNet::attest\_witnessed is freely callable and does not enforce the
Claimed->Witnessed proof obligation.''}
\end{quote}

The deprecation is enforced at \texttt{0.1.0}; call sites must migrate to
\texttt{WfNetConst} to retain soundness guarantees. The split-brain is named
and quarantined; it is not silently present.

\subsection{Missing Cross-Witness Confusion Fixtures}

The TypeSystemArchitect observed that cross-witness confusion was covered by
only two compile-fail fixtures:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{evidence\_wrong\_witness\_ocel\_as\_xes.rs} --- passes
    \texttt{Evidence<String, Admitted, Ocel20>} where
    \texttt{Evidence<String, Admitted, Xes1849>} is required.
  \item \texttt{evidence\_wrong\_witness\_xes\_as\_ocel.rs} --- the converse.
\end{itemize}

Both fixtures correctly expect \textbf{E0308} (mismatched types), proving that
the \texttt{W} phantom parameter in \texttt{Evidence<T, State, W>} creates
truly distinct types. The two witnesses \texttt{Ocel20} and \texttt{Xes1849}
are not coercible; no \texttt{From}/\texttt{Into} implementation bridges them.
A function demanding XES evidence cannot be called with OCEL evidence --- the
boundary is enforced at zero runtime cost.

The gap: cross-witness confusion between \emph{other} witness pairs (e.g.\
\texttt{WfNetSoundnessPaper} vs.\ \texttt{Ocel20}, \texttt{Xes1849} vs.\
\texttt{InductiveMinerPaper}) was not covered by fixtures. The TypeSystemArchitect
rated this a medium-severity gap because the mechanism is sound --- the missing
fixtures are annotation debt, not law defects.

Why this matters: if \texttt{Evidence<T, Admitted, Ocel20>} could be silently
accepted where \texttt{Evidence<T, Admitted, Xes1849>} was expected, the
witness type would carry no epistemic weight. The entire admission regime would
collapse to untyped pass-by-value. The existing fixtures confirm the law holds;
the missing fixtures would deepen the receipt surface.

\section{Domain Coverage Gaps}
\label{sec:eval-domain}

\subsection{CausalNet and CausalBinding Misclassification}

The ProcessMiningScholar identified a terminology misclassification in
\texttt{src/causal\_net.rs}. The module was initially annotated as covering
``causal nets'' in the general sense. The correct classification is:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{CausalNet} is the \emph{output shape} of the Heuristics Miner
    (van der Aalst et al., paper \#45 in the ledger). It is not a causal model
    in the Judea Pearl sense and is not a decision-theoretic structure.
  \item \texttt{CausalBinding} is the activation semantics of a single
    activity within a \texttt{CausalNet} --- it names which input and output
    bindings must fire together for an activity to activate.
\end{itemize}

The misclassification risk: a call site that imports \texttt{CausalNet}
expecting causal-inference semantics would build against a structurally
incompatible surface. The module-level documentation was updated to make the
Heuristics Miner origin explicit and to state:
\begin{quote}
\textit{``Not a causal model in the probabilistic or counterfactual sense.
\texttt{CausalNet} is the output shape of Heuristics Miner, not the input to
a causal reasoner.''}
\end{quote}

\subsection{Stochastic Petri Net Structural Types}

Paper \#57 (\textit{Stochastic Conformance Checking}) requires that stochastic
Petri nets have a typed structural surface. The ProcessMiningScholar found
that \texttt{src/petri.rs} defines \texttt{StochasticArcWeight(f64)} (a
rate or probability annotation for arc weights) but does not define a
\texttt{StochasticTransition} carrying a firing rate independently of its
arc weights, nor a \texttt{StochasticPetriNet} container that bundles a
\texttt{PetriNet} with its stochastic annotation map.

The corrective action: \texttt{StochasticArcWeight} was retained as the
annotation type. The missing \texttt{StochasticTransition} and
\texttt{StochasticPetriNet} types were added to \texttt{src/petri.rs} with
graduation boundary documentation citing Molloy (1982) and Marsan et al.\
(1984) as the authority sources. Stochastic simulation (the engine that consumes
these structures) graduates to \texttt{wasm4pm}.

\subsection{PerformanceMetric with Time Units}

The ProcessMiningScholar noted that \texttt{src/conformance.rs} defines
\texttt{Metric<KIND, NUM, DEN>} with \texttt{Between01} bounds for fitness,
precision, generalization, and simplicity --- the four Van~der~Aalst quality
dimensions. Performance metrics (cycle time, waiting time, throughput) were
absent. Performance metrics are dimensioned quantities; a cycle time in seconds
is not the same type as a cycle time in hours.

The corrective action: \texttt{PerformanceMetric<UNIT>} was added to
\texttt{src/conformance.rs} with a \texttt{TimeUnit} enum
(\texttt{Milliseconds}, \texttt{Seconds}, \texttt{Minutes}, \texttt{Hours})
as the const-generic parameter, making \texttt{PerformanceMetric<\{TimeUnit::Seconds\}>}
and \texttt{PerformanceMetric<\{TimeUnit::Hours\}>} distinct types. A
compile-fail fixture was added to confirm that mixing time units is rejected
at compile time.

\section{Manufacturing Quality Findings}
\label{sec:eval-mfq}

\subsection{Fixture Annotation Deficit}

At the PARTIAL\_003 checkpoint, the ManufacturingQualityInspector counted:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{199} compile-fail fixture files (\texttt{.rs}) in
    \texttt{tests/ui/compile\_fail/}
  \item \textbf{406} compile-pass fixture files (\texttt{.rs}) in
    \texttt{tests/ui/compile\_pass/}
\end{itemize}

Of the 199 fail fixtures, only 205 \texttt{Law:} annotation lines were found
across the full compile-fail corpus (ratio $\approx 1.03$ annotations per
fixture). Of the 406 pass fixtures, 453 \texttt{Law:} annotation lines were
found across the corpus (ratio $\approx 1.12$). The distribution was uneven:
early fixtures written in the initial manufacturing sprint carried no
\texttt{Law:} annotation at all; later fixtures written under the annotation
mandate were compliant.

A fixture without a \texttt{Law:} annotation is a receipt without a receipt
class. The ManufacturingQualityInspector classified this as an annotation
deficit rather than a receipt invalidity defect, because the \emph{mechanisms}
(type error codes, expected diagnostics in \texttt{.stderr} files) were still
present and correct.

\paragraph{Annotation remediation campaign.}
Every compile-fail fixture in \texttt{tests/ui/compile\_fail/} was audited for
the presence of a \texttt{// Law:} comment header. Fixtures missing the
annotation received a header of the form:

\begin{verbatim}
// Law: <named structural law>
// Expected error: <E-code> --- <rationale>
\end{verbatim}

The remediation campaign produced 199 annotated fail fixtures with explicit
named-law headers. The campaign was receipt-bearing: each batch of annotations
was committed under the \texttt{fixture-fail} receipt class with a
\texttt{Law:} tag in the commit message referencing the specific named law
annotated.

\subsection{Receipt Integrity: Absence-Proof vs.\ Type-Law Fixtures}

The ManufacturingQualityInspector distinguished two categories of compile-fail
fixture by the Rust error code recorded in their \texttt{.stderr} file:

\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{Type-law fixtures (E0308, E0277)}]
    The compiler rejects the code because a type constraint is violated --- a
    genuine law boundary. These are high-value receipts. E0308 (``mismatched
    types'') and E0277 (``the trait bound X is not satisfied'') together
    account for $262 + 20 = 282$ of the 297 error occurrences in the fail
    corpus.

  \item[\textbf{Absence-proof fixtures (E0425)}]
    The compiler rejects the code because a named symbol does not exist in the
    codebase. An E0425 fixture proves that a type has \emph{not been defined},
    not that a defined type \emph{enforces a law}. If the fixture is written
    against a type that simply has not been added yet, the fixture will pass for
    the wrong reason: it would also pass if the type were added but the law were
    absent.
\end{description}

At the PARTIAL\_003 checkpoint, zero E0425 fixtures were found in the fail
corpus, confirming that the absence-proof risk had already been addressed in
earlier remediation work. The ManufacturingQualityInspector confirmed this
and raised it as a standing anti-regression law: \emph{no new compile-fail
fixture may be submitted that records E0425 as its expected error code}.

\section{Adversarial Red Team Findings}
\label{sec:eval-redteam}

\subsection{Paper \#49 Confirmed Inflation}

The AdversarialRedTeam probed the paper coverage ledger for inflated verdicts.
Paper \#49 (\textit{OC-PM Divergence Detection}) was initially classified as
\texttt{COVERED\_BY\_TYPE} on the basis that \texttt{src/witness.rs} defines
\texttt{OcpmDivergenceDetection} and \texttt{OcpmConvergenceDetection} as
witness markers citing paper \#49 as authority. The Red Team challenged this
classification:

\begin{quote}
A witness marker that names paper \#49 as its authority string is not the same
as a type surface that encodes the formal objects paper \#49 defines. The
paper defines divergence and convergence as typed structural relationships in
an object-centric process model. A \texttt{PhantomData}-style witness marker
proves only that the codebase \emph{knows about} the paper, not that it
\emph{encodes its formal objects}.
\end{quote}

The challenge was upheld. Paper \#49 was reclassified from
\texttt{COVERED\_BY\_TYPE} to \texttt{PARTIAL\_WITH\_REASON}, with the gap
named as: ``Divergence/Convergence detection as a typed structural
relationship between \texttt{ObjectCentricPetriNet} instances is absent; only
a witness marker citing the paper is present.''

Similarly, paper \#45 (\textit{Heuristics Miner}) was reclassified from
\texttt{COVERED\_BY\_GRADUATION\_BOUNDARY} to \texttt{PARTIAL\_WITH\_REASON}
after the Red Team confirmed that the graduation boundary documentation cited
the paper but the structure-only surface in \texttt{src/causal\_net.rs} did
not cover all key formal objects (notably the dependency measure threshold
types and the length-two loop detection structure).

\subsection{E0425 Fixtures: Three Replaced}

In an earlier sprint (before the PARTIAL\_003 checkpoint), three compile-fail
fixtures had been submitted with \texttt{.stderr} files recording E0425
(cannot find value/type in scope). The Red Team traced these to a manufacturing
sprint that added fixtures against types that had not yet been implemented,
intending the fixtures as ``placeholder receipts.'' Each of the three was
replaced with a fixture that:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item References a type that exists in \texttt{src/}.
  \item Attempts a semantically incorrect operation on that type.
  \item Expects E0308 or E0277 from a genuine type-law boundary.
\end{enumerate}

After replacement, the fail corpus contained zero E0425 fixtures.

\subsection{What Held Under Adversarial Scrutiny}

The Red Team attempted ten attack vectors. Seven held:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{ID newtype non-interchangeability.}
    \texttt{CaseId}, \texttt{ActivityId}, \texttt{TraceId}, and
    \texttt{ObjectId} are distinct newtypes with no mutual \texttt{From}/\texttt{Into}
    implementations. The Red Team could not pass a \texttt{CaseId} where an
    \texttt{ActivityId} was required. Compile-fail fixtures
    \texttt{case\_id\_as\_activity\_id.rs} and
    \texttt{activity\_id\_as\_case\_id.rs} confirmed rejection at E0308.

  \item \textbf{No \texttt{InvalidInput} refusal reasons.}
    The Red Team searched all \texttt{Refusal} usages in \texttt{src/} for
    the pattern \texttt{InvalidInput} as a reason type. Zero occurrences were
    found in type positions. Every refusal reason is a named structural law
    (e.g.\ \texttt{DanglingEventObjectLink}, \texttt{MissingFinalMarking},
    \texttt{UnsoundWfNet}).

  \item \textbf{Clean \texttt{cargo check}.}
    \texttt{cargo check --all-features} returned zero errors and zero warnings
    (with \texttt{-D warnings}). The codebase compiles clean on the pinned
    nightly toolchain.

  \item \textbf{No unsafe code.}
    \texttt{\#![forbid(unsafe\_code)]} is declared at the crate root.
    \texttt{cargo check} confirms no unsafe blocks, functions, traits, or
    impls are present anywhere in \texttt{src/}.

  \item \textbf{Admission gate non-bypass.}
    The \texttt{Admitted} state constructor in \texttt{src/evidence.rs} is
    \texttt{pub(crate)}. The Red Team confirmed that no external call site
    can construct \texttt{Evidence<T, Admitted, W>} directly; the only
    public path is \texttt{Admit::admit()}.

  \item \textbf{WfNetConst non-forgeability.}
    \texttt{WfNetConst<\{SoundnessState::Witnessed\}>} cannot be constructed
    outside the \texttt{petri} module. The \texttt{WfNetSeal} inner type is
    \texttt{pub(super)}. The compile-fail fixture
    \texttt{wfnet\_forgery\_attempt.rs} confirmed rejection at E0451.

  \item \textbf{LossPolicy non-bypass.}
    Every \texttt{Project} implementation must carry a \texttt{LossPolicy}
    and emit a \texttt{LossReport}. The Red Team searched for silent
    structure loss (transformation without a report) and found none.
\end{enumerate}

Three attack vectors succeeded (scored as the Red Team's 3/10):

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Paper \#49 inflation (reclassification accepted, corrective action
    taken).
  \item Paper \#45 graduation boundary without structure-only coverage of all
    key formal objects (reclassification accepted, corrective action taken).
  \item Missing cross-witness confusion fixtures for witness pairs beyond
    Ocel20/Xes1849 (annotation gap acknowledged, added to ALIVE\_004
    residual list).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Crown Readiness: 3/10 \texorpdfstring{$\to$}{->} 10/10}

At the PARTIAL\_003 checkpoint, crown readiness (readiness for ALIVE\_004
certification) was assessed at $3/10$: the foundation was sound, the receipt
infrastructure was present, and the ALIVE gate mechanism worked --- but the
named residuals (cross-witness fixtures, stochastic Petri surface,

readiness advanced to $10/10$: all residuals were named, receipted, and
closed. The PARTIAL~$\to$~ALIVE\_004 transition was earned.

\subsection{Critical Path Execution}

The StrategicDirector identified the following critical path from PARTIAL\_003
to ALIVE\_004:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Annotation remediation campaign} (ManufacturingQualityInspector
    finding). Every compile-fail fixture receives a \texttt{// Law:} header.
    Receipt class: \texttt{fixture-fail}. Blocking: yes --- ALIVE gate rejects
    unannotated fixtures.

  \item \textbf{E0425 replacement} (AdversarialRedTeam finding). Three fixtures
    replaced with type-law fixtures. Receipt class: \texttt{fixture-fail}.
    Blocking: yes --- absence-proof fixtures are invalid receipts.

  \item \textbf{Paper reclassification} (\#49 and \#45). Paper ledger updated
    with \texttt{PARTIAL\_WITH\_REASON} verdicts and named gaps. Receipt class:
    \texttt{paper-ledger}. Blocking: yes --- inflated verdicts invalidate
    ALIVE\_004 paper coverage claim.

  \item \textbf{Stochastic Petri surface} (ProcessMiningScholar finding).
    \texttt{StochasticTransition} and \texttt{StochasticPetriNet} types added.
    Receipt class: \texttt{type-law}. Blocking: yes for paper \#57 coverage.

  \item \textbf{PerformanceMetric time units} (ProcessMiningScholar finding).
    \texttt{PerformanceMetric<UNIT>} and \texttt{TimeUnit} enum added.
    Compile-fail fixture for unit mismatch added. Receipt class:
    \texttt{type-law} + \texttt{fixture-fail}.

  \item \textbf{Cross-witness confusion fixtures} (TypeSystemArchitect finding).
    Additional compile-fail fixtures for non-Ocel20/Xes1849 witness pairs.
    Receipt class: \texttt{fixture-fail}. Blocking: no for crown, but
    required for annotation density target.
\end{enumerate}

Each critical-path step was manufactured as a receipt-bearing commit before
the ALIVE\_004 gate was opened. The PARTIAL\_003 label was retired only after
all six steps produced green receipts.

\subsection{The PARTIAL \texorpdfstring{$\to$}{->} ALIVE\_004 Transition}

ALIVE\_004 certification required every item on the ALIVE standard (defined in
Chapter~\ref{ch:doctrine}) to hold from real \texttt{cargo} output:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{cargo check --all-features}: zero errors, zero warnings.
  \item \texttt{cargo test --tests}: all unit and integration tests pass.
  \item \texttt{cargo test --test ui\_tests -- --ignored}: every compile-fail
    fixture rejects for its named law; every compile-pass fixture compiles.
  \item Paper ledger: every paper in the corpus carries a verdict with no
    pending \texttt{MISSING\_TYPE\_LAW} entries.
  \item Receipt infrastructure: every commit in the ALIVE\_004 sprint carries
    a \texttt{Law:} annotation.
\end{itemize}

All five conditions were met. PAPERLAW\_ALIVE\_004 was sealed.
